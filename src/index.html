BVLGARI, A powerhouse timepiece
imbued with elegance

Michelle Bateman | presented by Bvlgari

 

Known as the ‘ROMAN JEWELLER
OF TIME’, Bvlgari watches marry a classic design
ethos with a distinctly Italian flair.

In his 10-part tome De architectura, the 1st century Roman architect, engineer and author Vitruvius famously articulated the three central features of a well-designed building. Known as the Vitruvian virtues, they are firmitas, utilitas and venustas – strength, utility and beauty. To look at classical Roman architecture is to see these abstract qualities made solid; they have stood the test of time for millennia.

It’s not only architecture, however, that can be said to bear these signatures. The combination of form and function in the Vitruvian virtues extends to technology, design and engineering at all scales, from the largest public monument to the smallest and most personal piece of jewellery.

When the storied Italian design house Bvlgari notes that it takes inspiration for the design of its timepieces from the linearity and details of classical architecture, it’s easy to see the connection.

According to Bani McSpedden, Watch Editor at the Australian Financial Review, Bvlgari Swiss made watches are distinguished by creativity, which infuses in the craftsmanship.

“The accent is on the look and feel as much as the mechanicals,” he notes.

Leading
the field

Bvlgari was founded in 1884 as a luxury jewellery and accessories maker and added timepieces to its collection a century later. Headquartered in Neuchâtel, Switzerland, Bvlgari Time is uniquely poised to marry the distinct sense of Italian style with Swiss technical brilliance.

For watch aficionados like McSpedden, the arrival of Jean-Christophe Babin as Bvlgari CEO in 2013 ushered in a new era for the company. “In the case of Bvlgari its resurgence under Jean-Christophe Babin has seen its reputation in watch circles reach unprecedented heights,” he says.

Everyday Luxury

Perhaps nowhere is this more evident than in the Octo Finissimo collection, which has garnered more than 50 awards and six World Records globally since its introduction in 2014. The thinness of these watches is immediately apparent and was responsible for sparking a new trend for ultra-thin watches.

The size, however, belies the substantial mechanical brilliance that underpins these styles. These are ultra-performance timepieces that prove that the most luxurious gesture must also include an element of practicality.

The newest iteration – the Octo Finissimo Automatic Satin-Polished Steel – is designed for everyday wear. It features the thinnest automatic movement and a water resistance of 100 metres, made possible by increasing the case just slightly to 6.4mm and adding a screw-down crown.

“It’s great for dressing up or dressing down so you can wear it from the tennis court to the boardroom,”

says Bvlgari’s Babin.

According to McSpedden, the Octo Finissimo is more than deserving of its attention and accolades, due to “its form and the obvious architectural references inherent in the case shape and construction, all underpinned by impressive mechanicals. It’s a mix that results in one of the few genuinely contemporary watches on the market; one that could never be mistaken for a model from the past. Leonardo da Vinci saw art and science as complementary; it’s a watch he might have designed.”

Sign of
the times

This year’s other noteworthy collection is Serpenti Seduttori, featuring one of the most iconic symbols in modern jewellery and watchmaking. Bvlgari’s Serpenti motif has adorned stylish wrists, necks and earlobes since the 1960s, and has infinite capacity for regeneration.

The Serpenti collection represents the modernisation of an emblem, which in its latest iteration, demonstrates the power of fusing the contemporary with the classic and proves its standing as an enduring icon. As one of Bvlgari’s most recognised styles, the Serpenti Seduttori watch is characterised by its distinctive flat case and hexagonal pattern inspired by the original Serpenti watches.

In the new tourbillon style, this posed a fresh challenge for the Swiss artisans working on these timepieces. In order for the tourbillon to fit inside the sleek, serpentine shape of the case, it was required to be reduced in size, shrinking smaller and smaller until the final model – unveiled at the 2020 LVMH Watch Week in Dubai – was announced as the smallest tourbillon in the world.

The solution is not only elegant, it’s also significant, says McSpedden.

“Smallest, lightest, thinnest... these are seen as desirable attributes in the watch world and have traditionally been ‘holy grail’ claims for watch brands from the oldest to the newest,” he notes.

Bani McSpedden

“The creation of timepieces that break such records attests to a manufacturer’s imagination and skill, and at the same time draws valuable attention to the individual piece.” To underscore the importance of the movement, it’s treated with rigorous design respect: rhodium-plated, hand-decorated with Côtes de Genève, perlage and beveling, and featuring one sapphire bridge.

The Serpenti Seduttori Tourbillon models are available in rose gold or white gold with diamond pavé and a leather strap, or white gold with diamond pavé and a full diamond bracelet.

The wider Serpenti Seduttori collection is also available in rose gold, white gold, yellow gold and stainless steel, with full pavé dial options.

As with all Bvlgari timepieces, they have a foundation in tradition that aligns with the ‘virtues’ of firmitas, utilitas and venustas. But it’s the contemporary details
— an air of practicality; an eye-catching motif – that truly make these watches
a modern masterpiece.
